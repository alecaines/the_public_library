On Proof


It is not uncommon for scholars to think of their respective fields of study as conversations. For example, in the fields of philosophy, history, and particularly mathematics, it is often said that when we do work, we are in dialogue with the scholars who preceded us. The standard by which we engage in this dialogue is argumentation. But what exactly is argumentation?

Well, that is a tricky question to answer---and not entirely the point of this blurb. But to provide some closure, we'll say that to argue for or against something means that if a peer were to confront us witha cnojecture, we could verify it to be true using statements we already know to be true. Truth in this sense is determined by the concept of satisfiability. If we can say that there exists one combination of statements that yields what was proposed to us by our peer, then we have satisfied their proposition.

You might still be wondering how we know that the statements we used to satisfy our peer's proposition were true to begin with. Well, we could keep climbing up the causal chain and find that others have also been verifying the satisfiability of conjectures others have made for a very long time. In fact one could imagine that the chain goes on forever. But this isn't the case. The most popular mathematical tradition practiced in the west rests upon a set of axioms, statements we agree upon to be true or given to us. These axioms are calledd the Zermelo-Frankel axioms and assume some statements that are fairly easy to coneive of, such as the Axiom of Extensionality:

   "Two sets of elements are equal to each other if they contain the same elements"

and the Axiom of Pairing:

   "Given two elements, xand y, there is a set A that contains both x and y"

There are also some axioms that are fairly obtuse, such as the Axiom of Regularity:

  "That there will always exist two sets whose intersection is just the empty set"

Conjectures that can be satisfied using the aximoms in Zermelo-Frankel theory are called theorems, and the things that are said of such theorems are called proofs!

Things you might see alongside proofs

Lemmas and Corollaries
You may see mention of funy words like "lemma" or "corollary" without much of a hint as to what distinguishes them from theorems. Succinctly put, "lemmas" aren't really things in and of themselves. Rather, a lemma is the instance of a known proof being used to assist in the proof (satisfaction) of a conjecture. Similarly, a corollary is not a proof, but a new conjecture, that "naturally" or "immediately" or "trivially" follows the satisfaction of the conjecture at hand. A corollary "begs the question" of the original conjecture.

If and only if
There are many different tactics or strategies people have come up with to satisfy different sorts of conjectures. Some tactics are most useful, and at this point standard, when seeking to satisfy a conjecture. One very common conjectures invovles "if and only if" statements. The proof for a statement involving an "if and only if" condition requires two arguments to be made. Consider the following statement, "A is true if and only if B is also true." To verify that this statement is true, we must be able to say that "if A is true, then B is also, consequently, true" and also that "if B is true, then A is also, consequently, true." Satisfying each of these conditions is what mathematicians have decided satisfies an "if and only if" statement.
